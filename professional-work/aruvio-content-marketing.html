Navigating Reputational Risk and the Waves of Public Opinion on Social Media

Posted on June 18, 2014 by Candice McCollough

Social media is how modern companies connect with customer, employees, investors and partners. Every moment is an opportunity to strengthen business connections, and each missed or bumbled opportunity brings the risk of weakening them. Connecting with people on such a public platform can be like steering your organization into very rough waters, knowing how to successfully navigate the storm is critical. Just recently Delta Airlines dealt with a brief controversy on Twitter when they posted a picture of the statue of liberty and a giraffe in response to the USA defeating Ghana at the World cup. Except there are no giraffes in Ghana, something that hundreds of clever twitter users were quick to quip.

While Delta’s giraffe gaffe seems harmless and easily remedied with a quick apology, the issue illustrates how easily controversy about your company can be stirred up without careful management and oversight of social media. It may seem like a task best delegated to the intern and forgotten about, a silly extra facet of modern business, but forward-thinking audit committee chairs taking an active role in managing the reputational risk of social media need to ask their audit committee these simple questions:

1.) What are the risks we face by engaging in social media?

Other than looking foolish by making public geographical mistakes, when you speak on social media, you speak to everyone. Commenting on almost any issue will cause backlash from offended groups, and opting-out entirely from social media isn’t an option either. 85% of customers expect businesses to be active on social media.

2.) If an incident occurs, how to we mitigate reputation risk?

Having a plan in place to handle social media controversies is key. Being watchful of reactions to your content and being quick to respond and apologize for mistakes is often all that is required. Ignoring or not being aware of a problem will only inflame the issue. 53% of surveyed twitter users said they would expect a response from a brand within an hour. That number jumps to 72% if the customer is waiting for a response for a complaint.

3.) Does the board have the technical know-how to effectively use and co-ordinate social media campaigns?

Taking active command in a social media campaign is the best way for high level managers and directors to monitor reputational risk. While it may be more efficient to off-load the work of social media to your marketing dept, understanding the basics of popular media platforms lets you dive right in when an incident occurs to keep communication in-line with your brand’s mission.

Often times, social media is an under-exploited resource. It provides an opportunity to show the humans behind your brand and build customer and partner trust. With active management and the oversight of your audit committee, taking the reputational risk of social media will pay out dividends of reputational rewards.
